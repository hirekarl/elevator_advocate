# Reflection 01: The Bronx Lived Experience
**Author:** Karl Johnson, District 17 Resident
**Date:** April 14, 2026

## 1. The Human Cost of Neglect

As a Bronx resident, I witness daily the consequences of a system that fails its most vulnerable. My building is home to seniors, a neighbor with one leg who relies on a motorized wheelchair, and children—all of whom are rendered immobile and oppressed for long stretches when our elevator fails.

The root cause in my building is technical incompetence masked as a solution: the elevator installed is simply the wrong type for this structure. The issues will never be fully remediated until the management company commits to gutting and replacing the entire system. 

It is heartbreaking to see neighbors—seniors, those with dementia, those with amputated limbs—reduced to "hoping and praying" that management decides to fix the elevators. People are literally confined to their floors for days, weeks, and sometimes months on end. This isn't just a maintenance issue; it is a crisis of mobility and human dignity.

This neglect is not isolated. Other buildings under the same management company have suffered through elevator outages lasting three months at a time. According to resident accounts, there was even a death due to elevator neglect at one of their buildings a few years ago. This pattern mirrors a broader, lethal crisis across the Bronx, where documented incidents—such as the 2015 death of a senior at Boston Road Plaza [^1] and a fatal 2021 accident in Mt. Eden [^2]—illustrate the extreme consequences of systemic maintenance failures in the borough.

## 2. A Gift of Service: The Advocacy Platform

I have wanted to develop this app for a long time. This project is my response to that anguish—a gift of service to my community developed during my AI-Native fellowship at Pursuit. The core vision is to transform a personal grievance into a scalable tool for collective advocacy and survival.

While I’ve tried to maintain a manual log in my phone’s Notes app, the frequency and scale of these failures make manual tracking impossible to sustain. The residents in my building need more than a log; they need an intervention:
- **Tenant-Led Accountability:** Ensuring failures are noticed and logged by tenants, not just management.
- **Persistent Public Record:** Creating a reliable, historical account of neglect that cannot be ignored.
- **Actionable Data:** Empowering residents to use these records for immediate, collective action.
- **Emergency Mobilization:** Providing a way for neighbors to be alerted and mobilized during a crisis.
- **Support Networks:** Keeping care providers and family members informed about their loved ones’ accessibility status in real-time.
- **Universal Accessibility:** Ensuring seniors, those with cognitive, visual, or auditory impairments, and Spanish-speaking residents can navigate the platform intuitively, even in high-stress or life-threatening situations.

## 3. The Gap in Current Systems

The city’s **PACT (Permanent Affordability Commitment Together)** program, which shifted the management of previously publicly managed buildings to private entities, has been criticized for slow response times and a loss of oversight [^3]. Residents continue to report that private management has not resolved the fundamental crisis of elevator maintenance in Bronx developments like Mott Haven and Melrose [^4].

Compounding this is the failure of official reporting tools. The city’s elevator complaints database is plagued by two primary issues:
- **High Friction to Log:** Filing a 311 complaint is a lengthy process that yields reference numbers that are easy to lose or miscopy, creating a barrier to consistent reporting.
- **Lack of Actionability:** The **SODA (NYC Open Data)** records are notoriously out of date, suffering from reporting lags and exclusion of real-time NYCHA data [^5]. This creates a gap between the "official" record and the daily reality of residents, making the data unusable for life-safety decisions.

## 4. Strategic Mobilization: The Path Forward

Launching this project requires the active participation of local representatives and community partners who have already demonstrated momentum around tenant advocacy.

### The "Constituent-First" Wedge
Our strategy leverages my position as a resident of District 17 to gain immediate traction with Councilmember **Justin Sanchez**. This approach transforms a personal experience into a scalable legislative tool through **Data-Driven Legislative Briefings**. These reports replace high-friction 311 logs with quantified **Loss of Service (LoS)** metrics, triggering direct DOB inquiries and formal management accountability.

### Building the "Power Block"
We aim to empower a cross-borough alliance between the "Bronx Power Block" (**Althea Stevens, Amanda Farías**) and city-wide leaders like **Chris Banks** (Public Housing Chair) and **Crystal Hudson** (General Welfare Chair).

### Grassroots and Legal Integration
We are strategically aligning with:
- **CASA and Mothers on the Move:** To integrate our reporting into existing tenant union organizing efforts.
- **Mobilization for Justice (MFJ):** To ensure every hour of "Loss of Service" logged is legally admissible in Housing Court.

## 5. Conclusion: Dignity through Data

The ultimate impact of the Elevator Advocacy Platform is not just about technology; it is about restoring dignity to residents who have been stripped of their mobility and voice. We are grounded in the harsh reality that an app cannot physically repair a broken motor, but we are aspirational in our belief that **data is power**.

By transforming the "hope and pray" cycle into a transparent, undeniable record of neglect, we move from isolation to community and from anecdote to evidence. We are building a future where accessibility is not a luxury or a whim of management, but a fundamental right.
